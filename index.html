What is Bharatanāṭyam?

Bharatanāṭyam is one of the oldest classical dance forms of India, its grammar laid down in the Nāṭyaśāstra attributed to the sage Bharata (c. 200 BCE – 200 CE) and refined a millennium later in Nandikeśvara's Abhinaya Darpaṇa. It originated in the temples of Tamil Nadu as sadir, danced by the devadāsīs, and was reformed and brought to the stage in the early twentieth century by figures such as Rukmini Devi Arundale and T. Balasaraswati.

The dance unfolds in two complementary registers — nṛtta, pure rhythmic movement, and nṛtya, expressive narrative gesture. Its vocabulary is precise: every step, every glance, every shape of the hand carries a definite meaning.

And what are mudrās?

Mudrā ("seal") — also called hasta in the dance treatises — is the precise configuration of one or both hands. The dancer uses them to name objects, perform actions, summon deities, and trace whole stories without uttering a word.

Each gesture carries many viniyogas (applications). The flat-handed Patāka can be a cloud, a forest, a river, a wave, the act of denial, or the start of a dance — fixed by context, by the dancer's gaze, and by the rasa being evoked. The two great families are the twenty-eight Asamyukta and the twenty-four Samyukta hastas, from which the Devatā and Daśāvatāra hastas are built.
